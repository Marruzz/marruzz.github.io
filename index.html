Understanding Justice

Justice is a concept of moral rightness based on ethics, rationality, law, natural law, religion, or equity. It is also the act of being just and/or fair.

The concept of justice differs in every culture. An early theory of justice was set out by the Ancient Greek philosopher Plato in his work The Republic. Throughout history various theories have been established.

"Justice is the first virtue of social institutions, as truth is of systems of thought." — John Rawls

Principles of Justice

Rule of Law

The principle that all people and institutions are subject to and accountable to law that is fairly applied and enforced.

Fairness

Impartial and just treatment without favoritism or discrimination, giving each person their due.

Equality

The state of being equal, especially in status, rights, and opportunities regardless of background.

Mercy

Compassion or forgiveness shown toward someone whom it is within one's power to punish or harm.

History of Justice

Ancient Times

Early concepts of justice in ancient civilizations like Egypt, Mesopotamia, and Greece, where justice was often tied to religious beliefs and seen as divine will.

Middle Ages

During this period, religious institutions played a major role in administering justice, with concepts like divine judgment influencing legal proceedings.

Enlightenment

Philosophers like Locke, Rousseau, and Kant developed theories of justice based on reason and natural rights, influencing modern legal systems.

Modern Era

Contemporary theories of justice including distributive justice, restorative justice, and social justice have shaped our current understanding and institutions.

"Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere."

— Martin Luther King Jr.
